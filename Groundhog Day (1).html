data_sheet = xw.Book('groundhog_day.xlsx').sheets[0]

I imported the average 2-month temperatures in Punxsutawney, PA (most famous Groundhog's Day event) going back to 1901 from https://www.ncdc.noaa.gov/. These temperatures are given as the temperature difference compared to the average temperature between 1980-2018 for that same area.

I also determined whether or not the groundhog saw his shadow from Punxsutawney's website http://www.groundhog.org.

from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression
import numpy as np

linreg = LinearRegression();
linreg.fit(np.asarray(df['Year']).reshape(-1,1), np.asarray(df['Avg 2mo Temp Anomaly']).reshape(-1,1));
In [13]:
y_pred = linreg.predict(np.asarray(df['Year']).reshape(-1,1))
In [14]:
df['Expected Value'] = y_pred
In [15]:
ax = df.plot(x='Year');
ax.legend(loc='center', bbox_to_anchor=(1.25,0.5));
In [16]:
df['Temp Anomaly'] = df['Avg 2mo Temp Anomaly'] - df['Expected Value']
In [17]:
df['Avg Temp Trendline'] = 0

I have subtracted each value from its expected value based on the regression above. This effectively gives the data a mean of zero and allows us to compare a summer/winter temperature from 1901 to the summer/winter temperature in 2018. Check it out.

What does this mean?¶

Well, when the groundhog does not see his shadow, he has correctly predicted the coming of spring 9/17 times, or 52.9% of the time.

When he sees his shadow, he correctly predicted more winter 50/101 times, or 49.5% of the time.

TL;DR ==> Don't take Punxsutawney Phil to Vegas and expect anything better than a coin-flip!
